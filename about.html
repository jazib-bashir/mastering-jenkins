ABOUT

Artifact management solutions such as Artifactory, Origin, or Nexus have circled the DevOps universe for some time now. These solutions provide a number of valuable features including a centralized source for binary dependencies, a unified storage solution for intellectual property, a home for software tool assets and an easy way for non-engineering resources to procure a given release. To help clarify the solution an artifact repository is to binaries as source control is to source code.

This entry will attempt to codify known benefits of this type of solution, and encourage the adoption of this type of tool for those who are reluctant to implement.

Straw-man Arguments against Binary Asset Management Solutions

First lets address the straw man arguments surrounding the implementation of this type of solution. The more common argument from a development perspective is usually through the argument of

“I want everything available to me via a single checkout, and tracking binary files in source control works just fine for me”.

The practical implications of this perspective will lead to the following negative value returns:

Binaries are not managed & can expand into disk space realms that grow exponentially. Source control does a lousy job of differencing binary files and this leads to slower more painful checkouts Branches have no concept of binary assets and do not track the latest “blessed” binary as a dependencies. This leads to regressions and faulty releases.Binary tarballs or zip files are black box entities in source control (there is no understanding of where they came from, or where they are going). Binary files provide a unique way to hide defects. The second most common straw man argument is one of mythical lost internet connectivity and goes something like the following:

“But i want to build while not connected to the network”

Last time I checked there were wifi hotspots numbering in the millions. For the rare occasion that we do actually loose the ability to connect to the network, our ability to perform simple google (good research practice) also goes away. This in terms of active development means that one develops efficiently in a vacuum sandbox. Is that really a good development practice?
